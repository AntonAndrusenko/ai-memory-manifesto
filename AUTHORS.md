### Why this manifesto

I work with AI memory systems every day — as a product leader shipping AI-powered tools, as a researcher tracking the emerging AI landscape, and as a builder engineering agentic memory architectures from scratch. I maintain dozens of private agentic projects, including systems focused specifically on memory engineering — vector databases, knowledge graphs, semantic retrieval, context engineering, and conversation persistence. I run automated research pipelines that surface new papers, GitHub initiatives, emerging startups, and shifts in the AI infrastructure landscape on a daily basis.

But the most vivid experience came from building my own personal AI companion — an agent called Max with a full memory system, emotional intelligence, voice, and a unique personality. It recalls facts, tracks context arcs, picks up on patterns, and maintains continuity across conversations in ways that already outperform human memory in some dimensions. Living with that system daily is what made the stakes real to me: when an AI remembers you that well, the question of who controls that memory stops being abstract.

This depth of exposure is what made the problem impossible to ignore. I spend my days inside the technical layers where memory actually lives — the infrastructure decisions that determine what an AI system remembers, how long it keeps it, and who controls it. I read the papers, experiment with the models, evaluate the retrieval systems, and watch new memory-related projects appear every week. From that vantage point, I can see something that is not yet widely understood: AI memory is becoming one of the most consequential layers of digital infrastructure, and almost no one is building it for the user.

Every major AI provider is building persistent memory. They are building it to make their systems stickier. The user gets personalization; the provider gets lock-in. There is no portable format, no meaningful export, no right to inspect what the system has inferred about you, and no way to carry your accumulated context to a competing service.

This manifesto exists because I believe the people building these systems — people like me — have a responsibility to demand better. The technical standards are not yet fixed. The norms are not yet settled. This is the window to define what users are owed before memory lock-in becomes irreversible.
