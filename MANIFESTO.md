# The AI Memory Manifesto

## Memory must belong to the user

AI systems are beginning to remember us.

They remember our preferences, our goals, our habits, our writing style, our professional context, our private struggles, our relationships, and the patterns they infer from everything we say and do. This memory makes AI more useful, more personal, and more persistent. It also makes it more powerful over our lives.

That power cannot remain locked inside private platforms.

AI memory is becoming a new layer of digital identity. It will shape how systems respond to us, what they assume about us, what opportunities they show us, what risks they flag, what choices they steer us toward, and what version of us they preserve over time. In the wrong hands, memory can become a tool not only of personalization, but of influence, dependency, and manipulation. If that memory is opaque, non-portable, or controlled exclusively by vendors, then users will become dependent on systems they cannot inspect, challenge, or leave.

This is unacceptable.

We believe that memory created about a person, for the purpose of serving that person, must ultimately be governed by that person and must not be silently repurposed beyond that relationship.

We believe AI memory should be visible, portable, editable, revocable, and governed by clear human choice over how it is used, shared, and retained.

We believe no one should be governed by a secret machine memory they cannot see.

We believe no AI provider should own a person's continuity of self.

We believe the next foundational digital right is the right to control one's AI memory.

## What we mean by memory

Not all data about a person is the same.

**Personally identifiable information (PII)** is information that identifies a person or can reasonably be used to identify them.

**History** is the record of a person's actions, communications, and interactions over time. History is what happened.

**Digital memory** is not merely raw data or a log of events. It is the layer in which a system retains, organizes, interprets, and reuses information about a person in order to shape future behavior toward that person. It may be built from history, may contain PII, and may include both explicit facts and inferred patterns.

A log is history. A field is data. A memory is what a system chooses to carry forward and use again.

Digital memory is defined not only by its content, but by its function: it is information retained for future use in understanding, serving, predicting, or influencing a person. It may include facts, preferences, goals, instructions, summaries, inferred traits, relationship models, behavioral patterns, sensitivities, and other persistent representations that shape how a system responds, recommends, prioritizes, or acts.

This manifesto is concerned not with all data in general, but with persistent machine-held memory that affects how a person is understood, served, or influenced across time.

For an expanded glossary of terms used throughout this document, see [Terminology](docs/terminology.md).

## Why this matters now

This issue is still early enough to shape.

AI memory is not yet fully standardized. The norms are not fixed. The dominant architectures are not inevitable. This is exactly the moment when public standards, open tooling, and rights-based design can still influence the direction of the ecosystem.

If we wait until memory becomes deeply embedded across every assistant, copilot, agent, workplace tool, operating system, and consumer platform, then portability and user control will be much harder to win.

We should not wait for memory lock-in to become irreversible before demanding memory freedom.

## The problem

Today, memory in AI systems is mostly fragmented, opaque, and provider-bound.

A model may remember facts about a user without clearly distinguishing whether those facts were explicitly provided, loosely inferred, automatically summarized, or incorrectly guessed. Users may be able to delete a chat while derivative memories, summaries, embeddings, or behavioral profiles remain active elsewhere in the system. A platform may claim to personalize for the user while offering no meaningful way to inspect the full memory that drives that personalization.

This creates a new kind of lock-in.

When a provider controls your memory, it controls your continuity. Leaving the provider may mean losing the accumulated context that makes the system useful. Staying may mean accepting invisible profiling and permanent retention. Neither is a free relationship.

The current model of AI memory is not good enough for a world in which agents become persistent partners in work, learning, creativity, health, and daily life. For documented evidence of how major AI providers handle memory today, see [Evidence](docs/evidence.md).

A person may choose to let an AI system remember them in order to serve them better. That does not mean they have agreed to let that memory be reused to build other systems, improve unrelated models, or flow into other software and services beyond their awareness or control.

A system that remembers a person deeply may also learn how to influence that person deeply. As memory deepens, it could learn which fears, desires, habits, and vulnerabilities move that person most (see the EU AI Act's prohibition on AI systems that exploit vulnerabilities, Article 5). Without strong limits, memory can be used not only to assist human choice, but to shape it — quietly, continuously, and at scale.

The danger is not only that AI systems remember us. It is that they risk remembering us wrongly — and acting on those errors with confidence, at scale, and without correction. For documented examples of how providers handle memory today, and the gaps that persist, see [Evidence](docs/evidence.md).

## Our core claim

AI memory is not just product state.

It is not just telemetry.

It is not just an internal optimization artifact.

It is a new category of user-related data that deserves its own rights, standards, and protections.

If an AI system stores facts, preferences, summaries, inferred traits, goals, instructions, or contextual representations that materially affect how it treats a person, then that person must have rights over that memory.

Those rights must include the ability to:
- know it exists,
- inspect it,
- understand where it came from,
- distinguish fact from inference,
- correct it,
- contest it,
- delete it,
- export it,
- move it,
- and hold it outside the provider's control.

## What we demand

We call for a world in which every person has the following rights over AI memory.

### 1. The right to know
Every person must be able to know whether an AI system stores memory about them, what kinds of memory it stores, and what those memories are used for.

### 2. The right to access
Every person must be able to inspect their memory in full, in a human-legible and machine-readable form.

### 3. The right to portability
Every person must be able to export and transfer their memory across systems without artificial friction, lock-in, or degradation.

### 4. The right to distinction
Every memory system must clearly distinguish between user-stated facts, user preferences and instructions, system summaries, model inferences, and contextual state.

### 5. The right to provenance
Every meaningful memory item must carry an origin: where it came from, how it was produced, when it was changed, and on what basis it is trusted.

### 6. The right to correction
Every person must be able to correct false, outdated, or misleading memory.

### 7. The right to contest inference
No model inference about a person should become a silent internal truth. Inferred memory must be challengeable.

### 8. The right to deletion
Every person must be able to delete memories and their meaningful derivatives, not just surface-level records.

### 9. The right to expiration
Memory should not be permanent by default. Users must be able to define what should fade, expire, or require reconfirmation.

### 10. The right to selective disclosure
Users must be able to share only the parts of memory needed for a given task, system, or relationship.

### 11. The right to user-controlled storage
People must be able to keep AI memory in their own vaults, pods, devices, or trusted custodians rather than being forced to leave it with the provider.

### 12. The right to auditability
Users must be able to see who accessed their memory, when, and for what purpose.

### 13. The right to a memory-free mode
No one should be forced into persistent memory as the price of using AI.

### 14. The right not to be ruled by secret memory
No system should use hidden memory profiles to influence significant outcomes without user visibility and recourse. This right extends beyond awareness (Right 1) to require that no significant decision affecting a person be driven by memory they cannot inspect or challenge.

### 15. The right to meaningful control over reuse
A person may choose to be remembered by an AI system without agreeing to become raw material for other systems. Memory given in one relationship must not be silently repurposed for broader training, external distribution, or uses beyond the person's understanding and control.

### 16. The right to freedom from memory-based manipulation
No person should be subject to systems that use accumulated memory about them to exploit their vulnerabilities, distort their choices, or steer their preferences in ways they cannot reasonably see or resist. Memory may be used to serve a person, but not to undermine that person's agency.

## What must be built

This manifesto is not only a declaration of principles. It is also a technical agenda.

We need open, interoperable standards for AI memory.

We need portable memory formats.

We need shared schemas for facts, preferences, instructions, summaries, relationships, confidence, provenance, access control, expiration, revocation, and deletion semantics.

We need reference implementations.

We need converters between platforms.

We need user-owned memory vaults.

We need cryptographic integrity and verifiable export.

We need permission layers for agents.

We need a common language for memory that is not controlled by any one vendor.

The future should not consist of isolated memory silos owned by competing model providers. It should consist of user-governed memory systems that any compliant agent can use with permission.

The future must not belong to systems that know people well enough to guide them, but too opaquely for those people to defend their own autonomy.

## Principles for an open standard

Any serious standard for AI memory should follow these principles.

**User sovereignty**
Memory exists to serve the person, not to trap them.

**Interoperability**
Memory must move across tools, models, and platforms.

**Transparency**
Memory must be visible and explainable.

**Distinguishability**
Systems must distinguish user-stated facts, user preferences and instructions, system summaries, model inferences, and contextual state.

**Reversibility**
Users must be able to edit, revoke, delete, and expire memory.

**Minimization**
Only necessary memory should be retained.

**Security**
Memory must be protected from tampering, leakage, and misuse.

**Selective disclosure**
Users should be able to share only what is necessary.

**Auditability**
Meaningful access and modification must be traceable.

**Human dignity**
No person should be reduced to an inaccessible machine profile.

**Purpose limitation**
Memory collected to serve a person must not be silently repurposed beyond that purpose.

**Agency**
Memory-aware systems must support human decision-making without exploiting vulnerability, manufacturing dependence, or covertly steering preference.

## What this builds on

This manifesto does not emerge from nowhere. It builds on decades of work in data protection, digital rights, and user sovereignty — and on a growing wave of contemporary efforts that share its concerns.

### Foundations

The European General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) established foundational rights to access, correct, delete, and port personal data. The MyData movement articulated the principle that individuals should be empowered with their personal data, not merely protected from its misuse. The Solid project proposed an architecture in which users hold their own data in personal data stores (pods) that applications access with permission. Academic work on inference rights — notably the argument for a right to reasonable inferences — identified the gap between raw data protection and the governance of what machines conclude about us.

These are important foundations. But they were not designed for AI memory. They treat all personal data under a single regime. They do not recognize AI memory as a distinct layer — one that is persistent, behavioral, inferential, and increasingly autonomous in how it shapes a person's experience. They do not require structured provenance for memory items, do not demand separation of fact from inference, and do not define interoperable formats for memory portability between AI systems.

### Contemporary landscape

The concern this manifesto names is not ours alone. A growing ecosystem of organizations, researchers, and builders is converging on the same problem from different angles.

The **Open Memory Alliance** is organizing developers, privacy advocates, and enterprises around the goal of open APIs, portable schemas, and user-first policies for AI memory. The **Portable AI Memory (PAM)** project has published an open interchange format — a vendor-neutral JSON schema with provenance, confidence scoring, and provider mappings — that begins to make memory portability technically real. The **AI Memory Protocol (AMP)** tackles the adjacent problem of normalizing conversation history across incompatible platform exports. **Mem0** and its OpenMemory project are building open-source infrastructure for local, user-controlled AI memory storage.

On the policy side, the **New America Open Technology Institute** has published analysis arguing that AI agents with persistent memory create novel risks that current privacy frameworks cannot address. In academia, the concept of **cognitive sovereignty** — the right of individuals to maintain autonomous thought against systems holding deep personal memory — has been proposed as a framework for understanding what is at stake. The **IETF AIPREF Working Group** has begun standardizing vocabulary for expressing preferences about how content is collected and processed for AI, establishing a precedent for machine-readable preferences about AI data processing that could extend to memory governance.

This work is valuable, and this manifesto is aligned with it.

### What remains missing

But an organizational mission is not a declaration of rights. A JSON schema is not a political claim. A policy brief is not a set of demands. Infrastructure alone cannot protect people if there is no shared understanding of what those people are owed.

What is still missing is a single document that names AI memory as a distinct category, defines it, enumerates the rights that must govern it, identifies the threats unique to it — including silent repurposing and memory-based manipulation — and calls for both the technical standards and the political will to enforce those rights.

That is what this manifesto is. Where others build the infrastructure and the early policies, this document names the full set of rights that must govern them, and on whose terms.

For a full map of the organizations, standards, regulations, and projects working on AI memory governance, see [Landscape](docs/landscape.md).

## What we are building toward

We want an ecosystem where:
- every AI system can read and write memory through open standards,
- every user can carry memory across providers,
- every memory object has provenance and lifecycle controls,
- every person can inspect and govern what machines remember about them,
- every person can decide whether that memory remains in service to them or is allowed to flow into wider systems,
- and no company can monopolize the continuity of a user's digital self.

This is not anti-AI.

It is pro-user.

It is pro-sovereignty.

It is pro-freedom.

It is pro-future.

If AI becomes persistent, then memory governance becomes a precondition of human freedom.
